The Beginning

Lets dive straight into the analysis and look at the amount of incidents happening in New York City.
Before we proceed it should be noted that the years 2012 and 2016 does not contain data for the entire year.
We have created two Histograms, where the one on the left shows all incidents each year in New York City.
The right diagram, shows the amount of incidents each year for a specific borough.
One can change the borough by clicking the checkboxes.

If we look at the diagram on the left, we can see an increasing trend in the amount of incidents in New York City each year. BUT! remember that 2012 and 2016 is pretty much invalid in this comparison since the incident count for these years are not representing the full year.
If we compare that to the diagram on the right, we can see that Bronx, Brooklyn and Queens have a slight increase,
whereas Manhattan remains stable. Staten Island however, see a decrease in the amount of incidents.

As one can also see there is a large difference between some of the boroughs. This is due to the fact,
that a borough like Staten Island is less populated compared to e.g. Manhattan.
An overview of the amount of people living in each borough can be found here.

But where are these incidents occuring?!?

Based on the histograms above we can describe somekind of correlation between the boroughs but not specifically where the incidents occur.
With a Choropleth Map we can see exactly this!
The map is divided into New York City zip codes and their respective amount of incidents from 2012 to 2016.
The colors are mapped to a given zip code and the amount of incidents that occur here. We have created ranges for each color and these can be derived from our indicator. If one desire to know the exact amount of incidents, the zip code or the actual name of the post office where the color is mapped to, it can be found by hovering the mouse over each area.

From the choropleth we can see that there is a concentration of incidents just south of central park in Manhattan.
There is also a concentration of incidents in Brooklyn.
It is interesting to see that the incidents are concentrated in these areas.
It could very well be due to the fact, that these areas are some of the most densely packed in New York City, with narrow roads and a large population.
These factors will most likely lead to an increase in the number of incidents compared to less densely packed areas.

I want to know more!

When discussing incidents in traffic there is a couple of questions which comes to mind:

  • What is the cause of these accidents?
  • Which type of vehicle was invovled?

The dataset used to create the diagram below has been divided into boroughs such that one can change inbetween these if desired.
The actual diagram is called a Bubble Chart and it answer exactly the two questions metioned above.
When Bubble 1 is selected, the diagram illustrates how many accidents there occured by a specific cause.
The bigger the bubble, the more incidents with that cause. (To see name of the cause, hover the bubble)

The other diagram, Bubble 2, is selected by clicking the blue button.
Here we answer question number 2 in the above list, Which type of vehicle was invovled?.
Just as with Bubble 1, the bigger the bubble gets, the more incidents occured where that specific vehicle was invovled.
Again, if one wants to see the actual vehicle that was invovled - hover the bubble.

When looking at the cause for an incident, it is clear that the most common one is Driver Inattention.
There could be many reasons for why driver inattention is numero uno of causes. It could be because there is ton of traffic and therefore hard to keep attention of everything around you.
The most odd cause that have a high count in Brooklyn, Manhattan and Queens is Lost Consciousness. How does one lose consciousness while driving?

Looking at the vehicle type invovled in an accident, the Passenger Vehicle has the highest count.
This is not really a surprise, since that is the most common vehicle on the road.

What about time?!

That is a very good question! And we have, of course, looked after patterns in the occuring accidents based on time of the day.
In specific we wanted to see if we could figure out at what time there occured most traffic accidents as well as find out if there was a specific time interval where people are more likely to get injured/killed in a accident.

The best way - to our knowledge - of finding exactly what we want is to use a HeatMap.
If Heatmap 1 is selected, the diagram illustrate the amount of incidents in each borough per hour basis.
The color of an rectangle will get more blue as the amount of incidents increases. And if that isnt enough!, one can use the indicator located below the diagram to look up what color maps to what incident amount.

If Heatmap 2 is selected, the diagram transist into showing the amount of people injured or killed. We are actually going as deep as to counting injury/kill count for people in a specific invovlement e.g. "Cyclist Injured".

Visualizing: Incident count in a borough at a specific hour or amount of people injured/killed

For Heatmap 1 we can see that most incidents happen during daylight.
It is mostly concentrated around rush hour which is from 8-10 am and 4-6 pm.

For Heatmap 2 people are mostly injured compared to killed, and it is again concentrated around rush hour.
It's a surprise to see that there are many cyclist involved in these incidents.
If one had the time, they could find data about cyclist in New York City and see if there is a correlation with our findings.

I want to know where these three incidents happen!

We do too, and we can try to predict it with the KNN model we have talked about.
Did you notice the colored text for the three incidents? If you did not, go take a look again. Have you looked? Good.
The following map will show where each of the incidents happen, represented with that specific color.
The prediction work by comparing incidents that happen close to each other.
You can change the amount of incidents it should compare to, by selecting either 5, 10 or 30.
We have found that comparing to the 10 closest incidents give a good prediction. Choosing fewer and the prediction will be less accurate,
and choosing more will not do much, other than have you sit longer and wait for the prediction to finish.
Naturally there is no difference in the time you have to wait on this web page! But know that there is a difference when you have to do the work behind the scenes.
